## Dyslexia-Friendly Design Principles (Research-Backed)

### Typography

**âœ… What helps:**
- Simple fonts (Arial, Verdana, SF Pro)
- Increased spacing between letters (+18% faster for typical readers, +26% for dyslexic readers)
- Increased spacing between words
- Sans-serif fonts
- Left-aligned text
- Larger font sizes

**âŒ What hinders:**
- OpenDyslexic font (research shows simple fonts often preferred)
- Heavy underlines
- Uppercase text
- Italics (except sparingly)
- Crowded letters
- Justified text

**Recommendation:**
- Default: Simple sans-serif (system font)
- Optional: OpenDyslexic (user choice)
- Large default size (18-20px)
- Generous letter spacing (0.05-0.1em)
- Generous word spacing (1.2-1.5x normal)
- Never justify text

---

### Colour & Contrast

**âœ… What helps:**
- Warmer background colours (peach, cream) reduce visual stress
- Dark text on light, pastel-toned backgrounds
- Avoid stark black-on-white
- User customisation (different preferences exist)

**âŒ What hinders:**
- Stark black-on-white (high contrast can be harsh)
- Flashing images/animations
- Dynamic, moving elements

**Colour palettes (British Dyslexia Association):**
- Light mode: Dark grey (#333) on cream (#FFFDD0)
- Light mode alt: Dark blue (#003366) on light yellow (#FFFACD)
- Dark mode: Cream (#FFFDD0) on dark blue (#003366)
- Dark mode alt: Light yellow on dark grey (#1E1E1E)

**Recommendation:**
- Default: Off-white backgrounds (not pure white)
- Warm tones (cream, beige, light peach)
- Gentle contrast (not maximum)
- Full user customisation (colour pickers)
- 24+ themes covering all preferences

---

### Layout & Information Architecture

**âœ… What helps:**
- Clear information structures
- Uniform hierarchy
- No "visual noise"
- Minimal information to memorise
- Clear headings and organisation
- Multiple pathways to same goal

**âŒ What hinders:**
- Large uninterrupted blocks of text
- Cluttered UI
- Inconsistent layouts
- Hidden navigation

**Recommendation:**
- Paragraph breaks (USFM format)
- Section headings (styled clearly)
- Consistent spacing
- Minimal UI chrome
- Progressive disclosure (hide complexity)

## Background Music Research

### The Evidence

**Overall finding:** Small but credible impairment (Hedges's g = â€“0.19)

**Nuance:**

**âœ… Music that may help:**
- Instrumental music (hip-hop lo-fi) - no credible hindrance
- For users who habitually read with music
- For users with ADHD (improves comprehension)
- Fast-tempo music for those with strong music preference

**âŒ Music that hinders:**
- Music with lyrics (â€“0.3 effect on comprehension)
- Pop music with vocals
- High-intensity music
- For users who don't habitually read with music

**The Attention Drainage Effect:**
- Distraction unconsciously reduces attention capacity
- Even when users aren't consciously aware

**Student perceptions vs reality:**
- Students believe music helps
- Achieve higher GPAs with music
- BUT participants were aware of detrimental impact of lyrics

**Optional, not default:**

  - [ ] Nature sounds (rain, streams)

  - [ ] Ambient pads (no melody)

## Page Turning vs Scrolling

### The User Divide

**Pro-Scrolling:**
- âœ… "Tricks into reading more" (endless scroll)
- âœ… One-handed reading
- âœ… More natural on mobile
- âœ… Less prone to accidental page turns
- âœ… No interruption (continuous flow)

**Pro-Page Turning:**
- âœ… Feels like real book
- âœ… Clear progress (pages left)
- âœ… Less "wearing down" fingers
- âœ… Simple tap (vs continuous scroll)
- âœ… Natural pauses (chapter ends)

---

### Research Findings

**Memory & Comprehension:**
- Paper readers had significantly higher recall vs Kindle (page-based)
- Scrolling may reduce spatial memory (no fixed pages)
- Page turning creates mental "landmarks"

**Technical Considerations:**
- Kindle e-ink: No scrolling (battery drain from constant refresh)
- Kindle app: Both modes available
- Apple Books: Both modes available

**User behaviour:**
- Strong preferences on both sides
- Depends on reading habits
- Device type matters (phone vs tablet)

## Design Recommendations for Scripture Scribbles

### Visual Design Hierarchy

**1. Typography (Most Important)**
- Large, readable font (18-20px default)
- Simple sans-serif (system font)
- Generous spacing (letters, words, lines)
- Paragraph breaks (USFM)
- Section headings (styled)

**2. Colour (Second Most Important)**
- Warm, gentle backgrounds (cream, beige)
- Gentle contrast (not stark)
- User customisation (pickers + themes)
- Dark mode (cream on navy)

**3. Layout (Third Most Important)**
- Generous margins (40-60px sides on desktop)
- Narrow column (600-700px max width)
- Clear paragraph breaks
- Section headings stand out
- Verse numbers subtle (margin or superscript)

**4. Animations (Fourth - Enhance, Don't Distract)**
- Gentle fade-ins (text appears)
- Smooth scroll (not jerky)
- Page turn animation (if enabled)
- Highlight animations (gentle pulse)
- NO flashing, NO moving elements

**5. UI Chrome (Fifth - Minimal, Progressive)**
- Hide by default (reading mode)
- Tap to reveal (toolbar, navigation)
- Auto-hide after 3 seconds
- Annotations in margin (collapsed)
- Click to expand

## Specific Design Elements

### 1. Fonts

**Body Text:**
- Default: System font (SF Pro on iOS, Roboto on Android, system on web)
- Options: OpenDyslexic, Atkinson Hyperlegible, Georgia (serif)
- Size: 18-24px (user adjustable)
- Line height: 1.6-1.8 (generous)
- Letter spacing: 0.02-0.05em (slight increase)

**Section Headings:**
- Weight: Semi-bold (not bold)
- Size: 1.5-1.8em
- Margin: 2em above, 0.5em below
- Colour: Accent colour (theme-based)

**Verse Numbers:**
- Reading mode: Margin (10px left) or superscript
- Subtle colour (50% opacity)
- Small size (0.75em)
- User can toggle visibility

---

### 2. Colours & Themes

### 3. Spacing & Layout

## Key Principles Summary

**What makes reading "beautiful":**

1. **Typography** - Large, simple, generous spacing
2. **Colour** - Warm, gentle, low contrast (not stark)
3. **Layout** - Narrow columns, generous margins, paragraphs
4. **Animations** - Subtle, purposeful, quick
5. **Simplicity** - Minimal UI, text is hero
6. **User choice** - Toggle everything, customise all
7. **Calm** - No flashing, no movement, peaceful
8. **Focus** - Distraction-free, progressive disclosure

**What dyslexic readers need:**

1. **Sans-serif fonts** (simple, clean)
2. **Increased spacing** (letters, words, lines)
3. **Warm backgrounds** (not pure white)
4. **Clear hierarchy** (headings, paragraphs)
5. **No visual noise** (minimal UI)
6. **User customisation** (everyone's different)

**What research shows:**

1. **Background music** - Optional, instrumental only, OFF by default
2. **Scrolling vs paging** - Offer both, user preference varies
3. **Universal design** - Designing for dyslexia helps everyone
